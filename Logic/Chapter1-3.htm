1.3 Propositional Equivalences

Propositional equivalences are used for simplifying logical arguments

Definitions - tautology and contradictions
Tautology

is a compound proposition that is always True

Column 3 of Table 1 illustrates that p ∨ ~p is a tautology, i.e., this compound proposition is always True no matter what propositional fact p is

Contradiction

is a compound proposition that is always False

Column 4 of Table 1 illustrates that p ∧ ~p is a contradiction, i.e., this compound proposition is always False no matter what propositional fact p is

Contingency

is a compound proposition that is neither a tautology nor a contradiction

 

Logical Equivalences

Definition - logical equivalence
The compound propositions p and q are logically equivalent statements if p ↔ q is a tautology.

This is denoted by p ≡ q

Example Using De Morgan's Laws

Example

p is "Miguel has a cell phone"
q is "Miguel has a laptop computer"

p ∧ q is "Miguel has a cell phone" and "Miguel has a laptop computer"

~(p ∧ q) is "Miguel does not have a cell phone and a laptop computer"

~p ∨ ~q is "Miguel does not have a cell phone or Miguel does not have a laptop computer"

~(p ∧ q) ≡ ~p ∨ ~q
